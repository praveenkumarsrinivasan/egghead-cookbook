    The Story of the Kamado & Big Green Egg

    3,000+ Years of Fire & Clay

    Long before gas grills and backyard smokers, ancient civilisations were cooking with clay. Clay cooking vessels dating back over 3,000 years have been found in China — simple, dome-shaped pots fuelled by wood and charcoal that trapped heat and smoke to cook food with remarkable efficiency.

    The concept travelled from China to Korea and eventually to Japan around 300–500 AD, where it evolved into a staple of Japanese kitchens.

    The Tandoor — Clay Cooking's Other Legend

    While the kamado was taking shape in East Asia, another clay cooker was making history further west. The tandoor — a cylindrical clay oven — traces its roots to the Indus Valley Civilisation around 2600 BC. Archaeologists have found clay oven remnants with signs of cooked food at sites like Kalibangan in India and Harappa in modern-day Pakistan.

    The word tandoor comes from the Persian tanur, itself derived from the Akkadian tinuru — meaning "mud oven." These ovens spread across South Asia, Central Asia, and the Middle East, becoming essential to cuisines from Punjab to Persia to the Caucasus.

    A tandoor is a tall, vase-shaped clay oven — open at the top, with charcoal or wood burning at the base. Food is either lowered in on skewers (kebabs, tikka) or slapped directly onto the scorching inner walls (naan, roti, lavash). Temperatures inside a tandoor can exceed 480°C (900°F), giving food that signature charred exterior and smoky depth.

    Skilled craftsmen known as Kumhars traditionally build tandoors by hand, using a special sand-free clay mixed with munji grass for durability. From Indian restaurants to Central Asian roadside stalls, the tandoor remains one of the most widely used clay cookers on the planet — a 4,600-year-old design that still hasn't been improved upon.

    The Mushikamado — Japan's Rice Cooker

    In Japanese, kamado simply means "stove" or "cooking range." The Japanese refined the clay cooker into the mushikamado — a round clay pot with a removable domed lid, typically found in southern Japan.

    The mushikamado was purpose-built for steaming rice: a pot of rice and water sat inside the sealed ceramic chamber, with charcoal below providing steady, even heat. Families used them for everyday cooking and ceremonial occasions alike.

    What made the design special was its thermal efficiency — the thick clay walls absorbed and radiated heat evenly, while the tight-fitting dome trapped moisture. The same principles that made perfect rice also happened to make incredible barbecue.

    From Japan to America — A Post-War Discovery

    After World War II, American servicemen stationed in Japan discovered these dome-shaped clay cookers. Amazed by the flavour and juiciness of food cooked in them — so different from the metal charcoal grills back home — many shipped kamados back to the United States.

    In the 1960s, airline pilot Richard Johnson designed and patented improvements to the mushikamado while flying routes to Japan. He added a grill grate, hinged metal bands, and a sliding draft door — turning a traditional rice cooker into a versatile barbecue.

    Word spread slowly through the southern United States, where low-and-slow cooking was already a way of life. The kamado had found its people.

    Ed Fisher & the Birth of Big Green Egg

    The real turning point came in 1974 in Atlanta, Georgia.

    Ed Fisher, a Navy veteran from Philadelphia, had travelled to Japan and tasted food from a kamado. He was hooked. Sensing an opportunity, he opened a small shop called The Pachinko Palace in Atlanta's Buckhead neighbourhood, importing two Japanese products: pachinko arcade machines and clay kamado grills.

    The kamados were originally a side business to fill slow seasons. Fisher's sales tactic was simple — he'd set up a kamado on the sidewalk outside his shop and cook chicken wings. The aroma did the rest.

    "It only took thirty minutes to make my first sale." — Ed Fisher

    As pachinko sales declined with the rise of electronic video games, Fisher went all-in on kamados. He looked at the round, domed cooker one day and saw exactly what it was — a big green egg. The name stuck.

    The Ceramic Revolution

    Those early imported clay kamados had one major problem: they were fragile. Exposure to rain, freezing temperatures, and general wear would crack them.

    Fisher knew the design was brilliant but the material needed to change. Through a connection at the Georgia Institute of Technology, he was introduced to advanced ceramics — materials originally developed for aerospace and NASA's space programme.

    In the mid-1990s, Big Green Egg stopped importing clay cookers from Asia entirely. They began manufacturing their own EGGs using a proprietary blend of advanced ceramics that could:

    • Withstand extreme temperatures (up to 750°F / 400°C)
    • Resist cracking from weather and thermal shock
    • Retain heat for hours with minimal charcoal
    • Last for decades with proper care

    The result was a cooker that preserved the ancient thermal principles of the kamado but with modern durability. It was a game-changer.

    Kamado & Tandoor — Ancient Cousins

    The kamado and tandoor evolved independently on opposite sides of Asia, yet they share a remarkable amount of DNA. Both are born from the same insight: clay and fire, sealed together, cook food like nothing else can.

    Kamado Tandoor
    Origin China / Japan Indus Valley / Persia
    Age ~3,000 years ~4,600 years
    Shape Egg / dome with lid Tall cylinder, open top
    Material Clay (now advanced ceramics) Clay mixed with grass fibre
    Heat range 90–400°C (low-and-slow to searing) 260–480°C+ (high heat)
    Strength Versatility — grill, smoke, bake, roast Extreme heat — naan, kebabs, tikka
    Fuel Lump charcoal Charcoal or wood
    Key principle Thick walls retain heat; sealed dome traps moisture Thick walls radiate intense heat; open top vents smoke

    What they share:

    • Clay construction that absorbs, retains, and radiates heat evenly
    • Incredible fuel efficiency — both cook for hours on surprisingly little charcoal
    • Distinctive flavour — food tastes fundamentally different from metal grills
    • Designs unchanged for millennia — because they got it right the first time

    The kamado excels at range — you can hold 100°C for an overnight brisket or crank to 400°C for pizza. The tandoor excels at intensity — its open-top design and extreme heat produce the blistered naan and charred tikka that no other cooker can replicate. Together, they represent two branches of the same ancient clay cooking tradition, separated by geography but united by physics.

    Why It Works

    The magic of the kamado hasn't changed in thousands of years. It comes down to physics:

    • Ceramic insulation holds heat with very little fuel
    • Sealed dome traps moisture for juicy results
    • Adjustable vents give precise temperature control — from 90°C slow smoking to 400°C pizza-oven searing
    • Lump charcoal burns clean with no chemical additives

    Whether you're smoking a brisket for 14 hours or searing a steak in 90 seconds, the Egg handles it all — just as its clay ancestors did millennia ago.
